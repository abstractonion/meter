# Culture Palettes → Product

Historical palettes inform accent discipline — not literal period recreation.

## Bauhaus primaries

Red, blue, yellow as **functional accents** on neutral fields. In product: one primary accent + neutrals; don't deploy all three at equal weight — pick a dominant.

## Japanese iki (粋)

Restrained palette, asymmetry, muted indigo (`ai-iro`) and warm paper tones. Product echo: low-chroma backgrounds, single indigo accent, generous gray.

## Art Deco metallics

Gold + deep navy + cream — luxury fintech, not developer tools. If the brand isn't luxury, borrow the **contrast ratio discipline**, not the gold.

## 90s web neon

A cautionary tale from design history. High saturation on large fields = fatigue. Neon belongs in 5% accent surface max.

## Practical extraction

When referencing a period:

1. **Extract hue + chroma relationship** — not exact hex from a poster
2. **Map to semantic roles** — which color is "action" vs "ambient"
3. **Test in dark mode** — historical palettes rarely had one; you must

## Symbolism checklist

| Color association | Product pitfall |
| --- | --- |
| Red = danger (West) | Red = luck (East) — know your audience |
| Green = success | Green = go only; don't color entire success pages green |
| Blue = trust | Default blue primary is invisible differentiation |

Semantic tokens (`--color-danger`) decouple symbolism from primitive choice per locale if needed.
